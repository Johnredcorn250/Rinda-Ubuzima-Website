Who We Are

From Projet Ubuzima to a Trusted National & International Partner

Founded in 2001, Rinda Ubuzima is a Rwanda-based health research organization and registered local NGO working at the intersection of clinical research, public health delivery, and community engagement.


We collaborate with the Rwanda Ministry of Health, national institutions, and international partners to translate evidence into practical health solutions — strengthening prevention, treatment, health promotion, and implementation capacity across Rwanda.

01
2001 · Foundation
Founded as Projet Ubuzima by Prof. Joep Lange of the University of Amsterdam.
02
2004 · Expansion
Activities expanded with Prof. Janneke van de Wijgert and the IATEC team.
03
2011 · Transition
Transitioned from an International NGO to a local Rwandan NGO under the name Rinda Ubuzima.
04
2014 · Official License
Officially licensed by the Rwanda Governance Board as a registered Local NGO.

Our Vision

A Leading Health Research Organization in Rwanda & the Region

Rinda Ubuzima aspires to be a leading health research organization advancing knowledge, delivery, and collaboration in health promotion, disease prevention, treatment, and public health implementation across Rwanda and the region.

Our Mission & Objectives

Turning Evidence Into Better Health Outcomes

  • Bring together researchers from all over the world to develop medical research in Rwanda.
  • Conduct and implement the clinical research and health studies to advance medicine forward and improve health outcomes across Rwanda.
  • Build and strengthen clinical research capacity in Rwanda to drive local medical advancement.
  • Promote health and well-being through community engagement and grassroots outreach efforts.
  • Collaborate actively with the Rwanda Ministry of Health and other stakeholders to design and implement effective programs that tackle public health issues.

Our Work

Selected Activities & Programmes

A look at how Rinda Ubuzima integrates research, on-the-ground implementation, and community trust to achieve meaningful outcomes in real-world contexts.

Community Engagement · 2025–2028

Twese Hamwe Program

Twese Hamwe is a national initiative implemented by Rinda Ubuzima in partnership with UNICEF and the Rwanda Biomedical Center to strengthen health systems and community resilience for outbreak prevention.The programme applies a One Health approach, integrating human, animal, and environmental health to address public health risks holistically across Rwanda. It equips frontline workers and communities to prevent, detect, and respond effectively to health threats.

Learn More →
HIV Prevention

Dapivirine PrEP Ring Study — Women-Centered HIV Prevention

Rinda Ubuzima has been working actively with the International Partnerships for Microbiocides South Africa on HIV prevention research, encompassing preparedness work for vaginal microbicide trials supported by EDCTP and sponsored by AMC-CPCD/Amsterdam. This partnership has expanded to include Phase II and III clinical studies assessing Dapivirine-based microbicides specifically a gel (IPM003 & IPM014) and a vaginal ring (IPM015)for HIV/AIDS prevention in women.

Learn More →
Digital Health · 2020–2021

e-ASCov — Digital COVID-19 Response

Supported district-level pandemic response by training Community Health Workers to use digital surveillance and referral tools. This programme shows our ability to combine field implementation, health worker training, and digital systems support in emergency contexts.

Learn More →
Clinical Research · Reproductive Health

RingPlus & KARISMA Studies — Reproductive Health

Advanced reproductive health research through clinical studies focused on safety, acceptability, and women’s health choice. These programmes underscore our role as a credible partner for research that is both scientifically rigorous and socially grounded.

Lessons Learned

What 25 Years Has Taught Us

01
Community trust is the foundation of effective research and programme delivery. Without it, participation, retention, and impact are harder to sustain.
02
Strong government collaboration helps research and implementation stay aligned with national priorities and increases the chances of lasting uptake.
03
Local-language communication and culturally grounded messaging improve reach, participation, and long-term behaviour change.
04
Adaptive delivery matters. Programmes that can respond quickly to disruption are better positioned to protect both quality and outcomes.
